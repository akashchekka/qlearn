Foundations

The building blocks of quantum information — qubits, states, and measurement.

Lesson 1.1

What Is a Qubit?

In classical computing, the smallest unit of information is a bit — a switch that is either OFF (0) or ON (1). Every email, photo, and app on your phone is built from billions of these binary switches.

A qubit (quantum bit) breaks this binary rule. Instead of being locked into 0 or 1, a qubit can exist in a superposition — a weighted combination of both states at the same time. We write this using Dirac notation:

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩

Here, α and β are complex numbers called probability amplitudes. They're not probabilities themselves — they're "square roots" of probabilities. The actual probability of measuring each outcome is:

  • Probability of getting 0 = |α|²
  • Probability of getting 1 = |β|²

Since probabilities must add to 100%, we always have:

|α|² + |β|² = 1

Concrete example: If α = 1/√2 and β = 1/√2, then |α|² = 1/2 and |β|² = 1/2. You'd measure 0 half the time and 1 half the time — like a fair coin flip, but governed by quantum mechanics.

Why complex numbers? Because amplitudes carry both a magnitude (how likely) and a phase (a hidden angle). Phase is invisible in a single measurement, but when multiple qubits interact, their phases can interfere — amplifying correct answers and cancelling wrong ones. This is the core mechanism behind quantum speedups.

Key Insight: A qubit is not "0 or 1" and not "0 and 1" — it's a precisely controlled blend of both, described by two complex amplitudes. This is fundamentally different from classical randomness because the amplitudes can interfere with each other. 
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector
import numpy as np

# Create a qubit in superposition
qc = QuantumCircuit(1)
qc.initialize([1/np.sqrt(2), 1/np.sqrt(2)], 0)

# View the state vector
state = Statevector.from_instruction(qc)
print(state)  # [0.707+0j, 0.707+0j]
print(state.probabilities())  # [0.5, 0.5]

import Std.Math.*;

    H(q);
    DumpMachine();  // Shows |0⟩ and |1⟩ with 50% each
    let result = M(q);
    Reset(q);
    return result;
}
Lesson 1.2

The Bloch Sphere

A qubit has two complex amplitudes (α and β), but the normalization constraint and the irrelevance of global phase reduce this to just two real parameters. That means every qubit state maps to a point on the surface of a sphere — the Bloch sphere.

Landmark states on the sphere:

  • North pole → |0⟩ (100% probability of measuring 0)
  • South pole → |1⟩ (100% probability of measuring 1)
  • Equator (+X) → |+⟩ = (1/√2)(|0⟩ + |1⟩) — equal superposition, positive phase
  • Equator (−X) → |−⟩ = (1/√2)(|0⟩ − |1⟩) — equal superposition, negative phase

The general state is parameterized by two angles, θ (polar) and φ (azimuthal):

|ψ⟩ = cos(θ/2)|0⟩ + e^(iφ) sin(θ/2)|1⟩

What each angle controls:

  • θ — the "latitude". Controls the probability balance between |0⟩ and |1⟩. At θ=0 you're at the north pole (all |0⟩). At θ=π you're at the south pole (all |1⟩). At θ=π/2 you're on the equator (50/50).
  • φ — the "longitude". Controls the relative phase between |0⟩ and |1⟩. This angle is invisible if you only measure in the standard basis, but it becomes critical when qubits interact — phase differences drive constructive and destructive interference.

Every single-qubit gate is a rotation on this sphere. An X gate is a 180° flip around the X-axis. A Hadamard is a rotation from the pole to the equator. Understanding the Bloch sphere makes gate behavior intuitive.

Key Insight: The Bloch sphere turns abstract linear algebra into geometric intuition. Quantum gates = rotations. Superposition = equator. Phase = longitude. Every single-qubit operation is a 3D rotation.
Lesson 1.3

Superposition

The coin analogy: Imagine flipping a coin. While it's spinning in the air, you can't call it heads or tails — it has some "potential" for each. A qubit in superposition is similar, except it's not randomness — the exact blend is precisely controlled and deterministic until you measure.

Equal superposition means 50/50 probability — you're equally likely to measure 0 or 1:

|+⟩ = (1/√2)|0⟩ + (1/√2)|1⟩ → P(0) = 50%, P(1) = 50%

Unequal superposition gives skewed probabilities. The amplitudes control how "much" of each state is present:

|ψ⟩ = (1/2)|0⟩ + (√3/2)|1⟩ → P(0) = 25%, P(1) = 75%

The exponential power: A single qubit encodes one superposition. But n qubits in superposition encode 2ⁿ states at once. This scaling is staggering:

  • 10 qubits → 1,024 states simultaneously
  • 50 qubits → over 1 quadrillion states (more than any classical supercomputer can track)
  • 300 qubits → more states than atoms in the observable universe (2³⁰⁰ ≈ 10⁹⁰)

The catch: You can't read all 2ⁿ values — measurement collapses the superposition to a single outcome. The art of quantum algorithms is designing interference patterns that funnel probability toward the correct answer.

Real-world analogy: Think of superposition like a library where you can search all books simultaneously, but you can only check out one book. The skill is in designing the search so the book you check out is always the right one.

Key Insight: Superposition gives quantum computers exponential state space — but extracting useful information requires clever algorithm design that uses interference to amplify correct answers. 
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector

# Equal superposition via Hadamard
qc = QuantumCircuit(3)
qc.h([0, 1, 2])  # H on all 3 qubits

state = Statevector.from_instruction(qc)
print(f"Number of states: {len(state)}")
# Output: 8 states (2^3), each with amplitude 1/√8
print(state.probabilities())  # [0.125] * 8

Lesson 1.4

Measurement

Measurement is where the quantum world meets classical reality. When you measure a qubit in state α|0⟩ + β|1⟩, something dramatic happens — the superposition collapses:

  • You get result 0 with probability |α|², and the qubit snaps to state |0⟩
  • You get result 1 with probability |β|², and the qubit snaps to state |1⟩

Three crucial properties of measurement:

  1. Probabilistic: You cannot predict which outcome you'll get on a single shot — only the probabilities are determined.
  2. Irreversible: After measurement, the superposition is gone. You cannot "un-measure" to recover the original state.
  3. Disturbing: Measurement changes the state. The qubit is now in a definite classical state, not the original superposition.

How do we get reliable answers then? Two approaches:

  • Repetition: Prepare the same state many times, measure each copy, and build up a histogram. After 1,000 shots on a 50/50 state, you'll see ~500 zeros and ~500 ones.
  • Amplitude amplification: Design the algorithm so the correct answer has probability close to 100% before you measure. Then one shot is enough.
Key Insight: Measurement collapses superposition — it's probabilistic and irreversible. The entire art of quantum algorithm design is arranging interference so the correct answer's amplitude is large before you measure.

Quantum Circuits

The language of quantum computation — how algorithms are expressed as circuits of wires, gates, and measurements.

Lesson 2.1

What Is a Quantum Circuit?

In classical computing, circuits are physical paths of electrical current through logic gates (AND, OR, NOT). In quantum computing, a quantum circuit is an abstract diagram that describes a sequence of operations on qubits.

A quantum circuit has three components:

  • Wires: Horizontal lines, each representing one qubit. The state flows from left to right as time progresses.
  • Gates: Boxes or symbols placed on wires. Each gate is a unitary operation — a reversible transformation of the qubit's state.
  • Measurements: Meter symbols at the end of wires. Measurement collapses the quantum state to a classical bit (0 or 1).

Key difference from classical circuits: Quantum gates are always reversible. For every gate, there's an "undo" gate. Classical AND/OR gates destroy information (you can't recover inputs from the output), but quantum gates preserve it. This reversibility is required by quantum mechanics.

The circuit model is the standard way to describe quantum algorithms. When someone says "run this algorithm on a quantum computer," they mean: compile the algorithm into a circuit of elementary gates, then execute those gates on hardware.

Key Insight: A quantum circuit is the "assembly language" of quantum computing. Every quantum algorithm — from Shor's factoring to VQE chemistry — is ultimately expressed as a sequence of gates on wires. Learning to read circuits is like learning to read sheet music for quantum computation. 
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit

# Build a simple Bell state circuit
qc = QuantumCircuit(2, 2)
qc.h(0)           # Hadamard on qubit 0
qc.cx(0, 1)       # CNOT: control=0, target=1
qc.measure([0,1], [0,1])

print(qc.draw())  # Text diagram of the circuit
#      ┌───┐     ┌─┐
# q_0: ┤ H ├──■──┤M├
#      └───┘┌─┴─┐└╥┘
# q_1: ─────┤ X ├─╫─
#           └───┘ ║

import Std.Diagnostics.*;

operation BellCircuit() : (Result, Result) {
    use (q0, q1) = (Qubit(), Qubit());
    H(q0);          // Hadamard on qubit 0
    CNOT(q0, q1);   // CNOT: control=q0, target=q1
    DumpMachine();   // View the entangled state
    let r0 = M(q0);
    let r1 = M(q1);
    ResetAll([q0, q1]);
    return (r0, r1); // Always same: (Zero,Zero) or (One,One)
}
Lesson 2.2

Circuit Notation

Quantum circuit diagrams use a compact visual notation that conveys a lot of information at a glance. Here's how to read them:

Wire labels:

  • |0⟩ or |1⟩ — qubit initialized to a known state
  • |ψ⟩ — qubit in an arbitrary or unknown state
  • q₀, q₁, ... — qubit index labels (common in Qiskit, Cirq)

Gate symbols:

  • Boxes with letters (H, X, Z, U) — single-qubit gates applied to one wire
  • Filled dot + ⊕ — CNOT gate. The dot is the control qubit, the ⊕ is the target. A vertical line connects them.
  • Filled dot + box — controlled-U gate. Control qubit decides whether gate U is applied to the target.
  • × — × connected by a line — SWAP gate, exchanging two qubit states
  • Meter arc — measurement, producing a classical bit

Reading order: Gates in the same column happen simultaneously (if they act on different qubits). Gates to the left happen before gates to the right. This left-to-right ordering corresponds to matrix multiplication in reverse — the rightmost matrix in the product is the first gate applied.

From equation to circuit: The equation CNOT · (H ⊗ I) |00⟩ translates to: two wires starting at |0⟩, an H gate on the top wire, then a CNOT from top to bottom. This creates the Bell state — the most famous 2-qubit circuit in quantum computing.

Key Insight: Circuit diagrams are the universal language of quantum computing. Every paper, textbook, and SDK uses them. Once you can read a circuit diagram, you can understand any quantum algorithm at the implementation level.
Lesson 2.3

Circuit Depth & Complexity

Not all circuits are created equal. Two circuits might compute the same result, but one could be dramatically more practical on real hardware. The key metrics are width, depth, and gate count.

Circuit width = number of qubits (wires). This determines how many physical qubits you need on the hardware. Current machines have 50–1,000 qubits, so width is a hard constraint.

Circuit depth = number of time steps (layers of gates). Gates on different qubits in the same layer run in parallel. Depth measures the sequential time the circuit takes.

Gate count = total number of gate operations. More gates = more opportunities for errors to accumulate.

Why depth matters most on real hardware:

  • Qubits lose their quantum properties over time (decoherence)
  • Each qubit has a coherence time T₂ (typically ~100 μs)
  • Each gate takes ~20–50 ns
  • A circuit of depth 2,000 takes ~100 μs — right at the coherence limit
  • Deeper circuits = more noise = worse results

Optimizing circuits: Quantum compilers (like Qiskit's transpiler) automatically reduce depth by: cancelling redundant gates, merging sequential rotations, and rescheduling gates to maximize parallelism. Circuit optimization is an active research area — even a 10% depth reduction can make the difference between a useful result and noise.

The parallelism advantage: If gates act on independent qubits, they can execute in the same time step. Three H gates on three different qubits have a depth of 1, not 3. Exploiting parallelism is how clever circuit design keeps depth manageable.

Key Insight: Circuit depth is the critical bottleneck on real quantum hardware. Shallower circuits finish before decoherence destroys the computation. A quantum engineer's job is to express algorithms with minimum depth.
03

Quantum Gates

The operations that manipulate qubits — rotations, flips, and multi-qubit interactions.

Lesson 3.1

Pauli Gates (X, Y, Z)

Just as classical logic uses NOT, AND, and OR gates to process bits, quantum computing uses quantum gates to manipulate qubits. The three Pauli gates are the most fundamental — each is a 180° (π) rotation on the Bloch sphere around a different axis.

X Gate — the Quantum NOT:

X|0⟩ = |1⟩ X|1⟩ = |0⟩

Swaps |0⟩ and |1⟩ — exactly like a classical NOT gate. On the Bloch sphere, it's a 180° rotation around the X-axis, flipping the north and south poles.

Z Gate — the Phase Flip:

Z|0⟩ = |0⟩ Z|1⟩ = −|1⟩

Leaves |0⟩ untouched but flips the sign of |1⟩. This doesn't change measurement probabilities at all (|−1|² = 1²), so why does it matter? Because the minus sign affects how qubits interfere with each other. Phase is the hidden lever of quantum computing.

Y Gate — Both at Once:

Y|0⟩ = i|1⟩ Y|1⟩ = −i|0⟩

Combines a bit flip and a phase flip. Used less often in basic algorithms but important for completeness — together, X, Y, Z span all possible single-qubit rotations.

Key property: Each Pauli gate is its own inverse. Apply it twice and you're back where you started: X·X = Y·Y = Z·Z = I.

Key Insight: X flips the value, Z flips the phase, Y flips both. Phase changes are invisible in isolation but drive interference — the engine of every quantum algorithm.
Lesson 3.2

The Hadamard Gate

If there's one gate you need to understand deeply, it's the Hadamard gate (H). It's the bridge between the classical and quantum worlds — it takes a definite state and creates a perfect superposition.

What H does — in plain terms:

  • Takes a qubit that's definitely 0 → puts it in a 50/50 superposition of 0 and 1
  • Takes a qubit that's definitely 1 → also 50/50, but with a crucial minus sign
H|0⟩ = (1/√2)(|0⟩ + |1⟩) = |+⟩
H|1⟩ = (1/√2)(|0⟩ − |1⟩) = |−⟩

That minus sign matters. Both |+⟩ and |−⟩ give 50/50 when measured, but when combined with other qubits, the plus vs minus causes constructive or destructive interference — which is how quantum algorithms distinguish correct from incorrect answers.

Self-inverse property: H · H = I. Apply Hadamard twice and you return to the original state. This means Hadamard can both create and undo superposition.

On the Bloch sphere: H rotates 180° around the axis halfway between X and Z. It maps the north pole (|0⟩) to the equator (|+⟩) and back.

Why it's everywhere: Nearly every quantum algorithm starts by applying H to every qubit. For n qubits, this creates an equal superposition of all 2ⁿ possible inputs — the starting point for quantum parallelism.

Key Insight: Hadamard is the "on-ramp" to quantum computing. It creates superposition (enabling parallelism) and its self-inverse property lets algorithms switch between the computational and superposition bases freely. 
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector

qc = QuantumCircuit(1)
qc.h(0)  # |0⟩ → |+⟩
state = Statevector.from_instruction(qc)
print(state)  # [0.707, 0.707] = |+⟩

qc.h(0)  # Apply H again: |+⟩ → |0⟩
state = Statevector.from_instruction(qc)
print(state)  # [1, 0] = |0⟩  (H is self-inverse)

import Std.Diagnostics.*;

operation HadamardDemo() : Result {
    use q = Qubit();
    H(q);            // |0⟩ → |+⟩
    DumpMachine();    // 50/50 superposition

    H(q);            // |+⟩ → |0⟩ (self-inverse!)
    DumpMachine();    // Back to |0⟩ with certainty

    let r = M(q);    // Always Zero
    Reset(q);
    return r;
}
Lesson 3.3

Phase & Rotation Gates

Pauli gates are "all or nothing" — each does a full 180° rotation. But quantum algorithms often need finer control: partial rotations and precise phase adjustments. That's where phase and rotation gates come in.

The Phase Gate Family:

  • Z gate: Adds π (180°) phase to |1⟩ — you already know this one
  • S gate (√Z): Adds π/2 (90°) phase to |1⟩ — applying S twice gives Z
  • T gate (√S): Adds π/4 (45°) phase to |1⟩ — the smallest "standard" phase rotation. The T gate is especially important because it's the key to fault-tolerant quantum computing and is often the most expensive gate to implement on real hardware.

Why does phase matter if you can't see it? Consider two qubits in superposition that are later combined. If their phases align (+, +), their amplitudes add up (constructive interference). If phases oppose (+, −), they cancel (destructive interference). This is how quantum algorithms selectively amplify correct answers.

General Rotation Gates:

  • Rx(θ): Rotates by angle θ around the X-axis — mixes |0⟩ and |1⟩
  • Ry(θ): Rotates by θ around the Y-axis — changes probability balance without adding phase
  • Rz(θ): Rotates by θ around the Z-axis — adjusts phase without changing probabilities

Universality theorem: Any single-qubit gate — no matter how complex — can be decomposed into just Rz and Ry rotations plus a global phase. This means a quantum computer only needs a small set of basic rotations to perform any single-qubit operation.

Key Insight: Phase is the "hidden variable" of quantum computing. You never see it directly, but it orchestrates interference — the mechanism that makes quantum algorithms faster than classical ones.
Lesson 3.4

Multi-Qubit Gates

Everything so far has been single-qubit operations — spinning one qubit on its Bloch sphere. But single-qubit gates alone can never make qubits talk to each other. To create entanglement and correlations, we need multi-qubit gates.

CNOT (Controlled-NOT) — the most important two-qubit gate:

If control = |0⟩ → target unchanged If control = |1⟩ → target gets flipped (NOT)

Think of it as a quantum if statement: the control qubit decides whether to flip the target. The truth table shows:

  • |00⟩ → |00⟩ (control is 0, nothing happens)
  • |01⟩ → |01⟩ (control is 0, nothing happens)
  • |10⟩ → |11⟩ (control is 1, target flipped)
  • |11⟩ → |10⟩ (control is 1, target flipped)

Why it matters: When the control qubit is in superposition, CNOT creates entanglement. The combination H + CNOT is the standard recipe for creating Bell states (Lesson 3.1).

SWAP Gate: Exchanges two qubit states entirely. |a,b⟩ → |b,a⟩. This can be built from three CNOTs. Needed when qubits on non-adjacent positions need to interact on real hardware.

Toffoli Gate (CCNOT): A three-qubit gate — flips the target only when both controls are |1⟩. It can implement any classical Boolean function, making it universal for classical computation. Combined with Hadamard, it becomes universal for quantum computation too.

Universal gate set: CNOT + arbitrary single-qubit rotations can build any quantum circuit. This is like how NAND gates can build any classical circuit — a small toolkit from which all complexity emerges.

Key Insight: Multi-qubit gates introduce conditional logic at the quantum level. CNOT is the fundamental building block — it creates entanglement when applied to superposed qubits, which is the basis for all quantum advantage.
04

Entanglement

Quantum correlations that defy classical intuition — the resource that powers quantum protocols.

Lesson 4.1

Bell States & Entanglement

Entanglement is the phenomenon that Einstein famously called "spooky action at a distance." It's the strongest type of correlation in nature and has no classical equivalent.

How to create entanglement: Start with two qubits in state |00⟩, apply Hadamard to the first, then CNOT with the first as control:

|00⟩ → H⊗I → (1/√2)(|0⟩+|1⟩)|0⟩ → CNOT → (1/√2)(|00⟩+|11⟩)

This final state is called |Φ⁺⟩, one of four Bell states. It cannot be written as a product of two separate qubit states — the qubits are fundamentally linked.

What does entanglement mean concretely?

  • If you measure Qubit A and get 0, Qubit B is guaranteed to be 0
  • If you measure Qubit A and get 1, Qubit B is guaranteed to be 1
  • This holds regardless of distance — the qubits could be in different cities
  • Each individual qubit appears completely random (50/50) when measured alone

Classical analogy (that falls short): Put a red ball in one box and a blue ball in another. Ship the boxes apart. Opening one reveals the other's color. But entanglement is fundamentally different: Bell's theorem proves that the qubits don't have predetermined values. The outcome is truly decided at the moment of measurement.

The four Bell states form a complete basis for 2-qubit entanglement:

|Φ±⟩ = (1/√2)(|00⟩ ± |11⟩) — same outcomes, correlated |Ψ±⟩ = (1/√2)(|01⟩ ± |10⟩) — opposite outcomes, anti-correlated
Key Insight: Entanglement creates correlations stronger than anything possible classically. It's not communication — it's a shared quantum resource that enables teleportation, secure key distribution, and computational advantage. Generating and maintaining entanglement is one of the hardest challenges in building quantum computers. 
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.quantum_info import Statevector

# Create Bell state |Φ+⟩
qc = QuantumCircuit(2)
qc.h(0)
qc.cx(0, 1)

state = Statevector.from_instruction(qc)
print(state)  # [0.707, 0, 0, 0.707]
# Only |00⟩ and |11⟩ have amplitude — perfectly correlated!

import Std.Diagnostics.*;

operation BellState() : (Result, Result) {
    use (q0, q1) = (Qubit(), Qubit());
    H(q0);
    CNOT(q0, q1);
    DumpMachine(); // |00⟩ + |11⟩, each with 50%

    let (r0, r1) = (M(q0), M(q1));
    ResetAll([q0, q1]);
    return (r0, r1); // Always correlated!
}
Lesson 4.2

Quantum Teleportation

Quantum teleportation sounds like science fiction, but it's a real, experimentally verified protocol. It transfers an unknown quantum state from one location to another — without physically moving the qubit.

What you need:

  • The qubit to teleport: |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ (Alice has this)
  • A shared Bell pair: one half with Alice, one half with Bob
  • A classical communication channel (phone, internet, etc.)

The protocol, step by step:

  1. Preparation: Alice and Bob each take one qubit from a shared Bell pair |Φ⁺⟩.
  2. Alice's interaction: Alice applies CNOT between her unknown qubit and her Bell qubit, then applies Hadamard to her unknown qubit.
  3. Alice measures: She measures both her qubits, getting two classical bits (00, 01, 10, or 11). This collapses the entanglement — Bob's qubit is now in a state that's close to |ψ⟩ but possibly rotated.
  4. Classical message: Alice sends her 2 measurement bits to Bob (by phone, email, carrier pigeon — any classical channel).
  5. Bob's correction: Based on Alice's bits, Bob applies X and/or Z corrections:
    • 00 → do nothing
    • 01 → apply X
    • 10 → apply Z
    • 11 → apply X then Z
    Bob now has the exact state |ψ⟩!

Important constraints:

  • No cloning: Alice's original qubit is destroyed during measurement. You cannot copy quantum states.
  • No FTL communication: Bob needs Alice's classical bits to correct his qubit. Without them, his qubit looks random. The speed of light is not violated.
  • The Bell pair is consumed: Each teleportation uses up one entangled pair. It's a one-shot resource.

Real-world applications: Teleportation is the foundation of quantum networks (a "quantum internet"), quantum error correction (moving logical qubits between physical locations), and distributed quantum computing.

Key Insight: Teleportation transfers quantum information using entanglement + classical communication. It doesn't transmit matter or energy — it transmits the state. This is the primitive that makes quantum networking possible.
Lesson 4.3

Superdense Coding

Superdense coding is the mirror image of teleportation. Where teleportation sends 1 qubit of quantum information using 2 classical bits + entanglement, superdense coding sends 2 classical bits using 1 qubit + entanglement.

The setup: Alice and Bob share a Bell pair, then part ways. Alice wants to send Bob a 2-bit classical message (00, 01, 10, or 11).

Alice's encoding — she applies one gate to her qubit:

  • To send 00 → apply I (do nothing) → state remains |Φ⁺⟩
  • To send 01 → apply X (bit flip) → state becomes |Ψ⁺⟩
  • To send 10 → apply Z (phase flip) → state becomes |Φ⁻⟩
  • To send 11 → apply XZ (both) → state becomes |Ψ⁻⟩

Each gate steers the shared Bell pair into a different Bell state. Since the four Bell states are orthogonal (perfectly distinguishable), Bob can tell them apart with certainty.

Bob's decoding: He applies CNOT then H to his two qubits and measures. The measurement result (00, 01, 10, or 11) is exactly Alice's original message.

Why this is remarkable:

  • Classically, 1 bit of communication carries 1 bit of information. Period.
  • With shared entanglement, 1 qubit of communication carries 2 bits of information.
  • The entanglement was shared in advance — it doesn't itself carry information, but it doubles the channel capacity.

The deep connection: Teleportation and superdense coding are "dual" protocols — they trade the roles of quantum and classical resources. Together, they reveal that entanglement is a genuine communication resource, as fundamental as bandwidth.

Key Insight: Superdense coding proves entanglement has real information-theoretic value: it doubles the classical capacity of a quantum channel. Entanglement is not just a curiosity — it's a resource.

Quantum Algorithms

Where quantum mechanics delivers computational advantage — from search to factoring.

Lesson 5.1

Deutsch-Jozsa Algorithm

The Deutsch-Jozsa algorithm is historically important: it was the first to prove that a quantum computer can be exponentially faster than a classical one for a specific problem.

The problem: You're given a black-box function (an "oracle") that takes n-bit inputs and returns 0 or 1. You're promised it's one of two types:

  • Constant: Returns the same value (all 0s or all 1s) for every input
  • Balanced: Returns 0 for exactly half the inputs and 1 for the other half

Which type is it?

Classical approach: In the worst case, you must check 2ⁿ⁻¹ + 1 inputs. For 100 bits, that's over 10²⁹ queries — more than the age of the universe in nanoseconds.

Quantum approach: Exactly 1 query. Always. Regardless of n.

How it works:

  1. Superposition: Apply Hadamard to all n input qubits, creating a superposition of all 2ⁿ inputs
  2. Oracle query: Feed the superposition through the oracle. Thanks to superposition, the oracle is evaluated on all inputs simultaneously
  3. Interference: Apply Hadamard again. If the function is constant, all amplitudes constructively interfere at |00...0⟩. If balanced, they destructively interfere, giving |00...0⟩ probability zero.
  4. Measure: If you measure all zeros → constant. Anything else → balanced.
Key Insight: Deutsch-Jozsa proves exponential quantum advantage is real. The playbook — superposition, parallel oracle evaluation, interference — is the template for all quantum algorithms that follow. 
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit

def deutsch_jozsa(oracle: QuantumCircuit, n: int):
    qc = QuantumCircuit(n + 1, n)
    qc.x(n)               # Ancilla to |1⟩
    qc.h(range(n + 1))    # Superposition on all
    qc.compose(oracle, inplace=True)  # Query oracle
    qc.h(range(n))        # Hadamard on input qubits
    qc.measure(range(n), range(n))
    return qc

# Constant oracle: f(x) = 0 for all x
oracle = QuantumCircuit(3)
# (identity — does nothing)
circuit = deutsch_jozsa(oracle, 2)
print(circuit.draw())

import Std.Diagnostics.*;

operation DeutschJozsa(
    oracle : Qubit[] => Unit,
    n : Int
) : Bool {  // true = constant, false = balanced
    use qubits = Qubit[n];
    for q in qubits { H(q); }  // Superposition
    oracle(qubits);             // Query oracle
    for q in qubits { H(q); }  // Interference

    // Measure: all zeros → constant
    mutable allZero = true;
    for q in qubits {
        if M(q) == One { set allZero = false; }
        Reset(q);
    }
    return allZero;
}
Lesson 5.2

Grover's Search Algorithm

Imagine searching an unsorted database of 1 million records for a specific entry. Classically, you'd check each record one by one — 500,000 checks on average. Grover's algorithm finds it in just ~1,000 checks. That's a quadratic speedup.

The problem: Given N items and an oracle that recognizes the target, find the target.

  • Classical: O(N) queries — linear in the database size
  • Quantum (Grover): O(√N) queries — square root of the database size

How amplitude amplification works (the core mechanism):

Start with all N items in equal superposition (each has amplitude 1/√N). Then repeat:

  1. Oracle step: The oracle "marks" the target by flipping its amplitude from positive to negative. Visually, in the animation, you'll see one bar dip below zero.
  2. Diffusion step: Reflect all amplitudes about their mean. This pushes the marked item's amplitude further from the mean (making it larger) and shrinks the others. Think of it as contrast enhancement.

Each iteration increases the target's probability by roughly O(1/√N). After ~(π/4)√N iterations, the target has near-100% probability.

Geometric picture: The state vector lives in a 2D plane spanned by the target state |w⟩ and the uniform superposition |s⟩. Each Grover iteration rotates the state vector by angle 2θ toward |w⟩, where θ = arcsin(1/√N).

Practical impact:

  • Database search: N = 10¶ → 1,000 queries instead of 500,000
  • Cryptography: Grover's algorithm halves the effective key length of symmetric ciphers. AES-256 becomes AES-128 equivalent against a quantum adversary — this is why NIST recommends doubling key sizes.
  • Optimization: Amplitude amplification is used as a subroutine in many other quantum algorithms

Limitation: This is provably optimal — no quantum algorithm can search faster than O(√N). Quadratic, not exponential.

Key Insight: Grover's algorithm provides a universal quadratic speedup for any unstructured search. It's not as dramatic as Shor's exponential speedup, but it applies to a far wider range of problems — anywhere you have "check if this answer is correct." 
from qiskit import QuantumCircuit
from qiskit.circuit.library import GroverOperator

# Oracle marks |11⟩ (target state)
oracle = QuantumCircuit(2)
oracle.cz(0, 1)  # Flip phase of |11⟩

grover_op = GroverOperator(oracle)
qc = QuantumCircuit(2, 2)
qc.h([0, 1])                          # Superposition
qc.compose(grover_op, inplace=True)   # One Grover iteration
qc.measure([0, 1], [0, 1])

print(qc.draw())
# High probability of measuring |11⟩

import Std.Arrays.*;
import Std.Diagnostics.*;

operation Grover2Qubit() : Result[] {
    use qubits = Qubit[2];
    // Superposition
    for q in qubits { H(q); }

    // Oracle: mark |11⟩ with phase flip
    CZ(qubits[0], qubits[1]);

    // Diffusion operator
    for q in qubits { H(q); X(q); }
    CZ(qubits[0], qubits[1]);
    for q in qubits { X(q); H(q); }

    let results = MeasureEachZ(qubits);
    ResetAll(qubits);
    return results;  // High probability of [One, One]
}
Lesson 5.3

Quantum Fourier Transform

The Fourier transform converts signals between time and frequency representations. It's one of the most important tools in all of science and engineering. The quantum version does the same thing, but exponentially faster.

What the QFT computes:

|j⟩ → (1/√N) Σ e^(2πijk/N) |k⟩

It transforms amplitudes in the computational basis into amplitudes in the frequency basis — exactly like the classical Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT), but acting on quantum states.

Speed comparison:

  • Classical DFT: O(N²) naively, O(N log N) with FFT
  • Quantum QFT: O(n²) where n = log₂ N — that's O((log N)²)

For N = 2³⁰ (about 1 billion), the classical FFT needs ~30 billion operations. The QFT needs ~900 gates. That's the exponential advantage.

Roots of unity — the math behind the scenes:

The QFT works with the N-th roots of unity: ω = e^(2πi/N). These are N points equally spaced on the unit circle in the complex plane. Controlled-phase gates implement the different powers of ω, creating the precise phase relationships needed for the transform.

The QFT circuit pattern: For n qubits, the circuit follows a systematic pattern:

  1. Apply H to the first qubit
  2. Apply controlled-R₂ (from qubit 2 to qubit 1), then controlled-R₃ (from qubit 3 to qubit 1), and so on
  3. Move to the next qubit and repeat with fewer controlled rotations
  4. Finish with SWAP gates to reverse the qubit order

The catch: You can't directly read the Fourier-transformed amplitudes (measurement is still probabilistic). But QFT is used as a subroutine inside larger algorithms — particularly Shor's algorithm and quantum phase estimation.

Key Insight: The QFT is the engine inside Shor's algorithm. It converts period information (encoded in phases) into measurement outcomes. Its exponential speedup over classical FFT is what makes quantum factoring possible.
Lesson 5.4

Shor's Algorithm

Shor's algorithm is arguably the most famous quantum algorithm — and the most consequential. It can factor large numbers in polynomial time, directly threatening the RSA cryptosystem that secures most internet traffic today.

The factoring problem: Given N = p × q (where p, q are large primes), find p and q. Example: 15 = 3 × 5 is easy. But when N has 600+ digits, no classical algorithm can solve this in any reasonable time.

Complexity comparison:

  • Best classical (General Number Field Sieve): O(e^(n¹˳³)) — sub-exponential, still impractical for large N
  • Shor's quantum algorithm: O(n³) — polynomial! A 2048-bit number would take ~2048³ ≈ 10¹⁹ operations

The brilliant insight — reducing factoring to period finding:

Shor showed that factoring N is equivalent to finding the period of a function f(x) = aˣ mod N for a random a. This period r reveals the factors:

If r is even: factors = gcd(a^(r/2) + 1, N) and gcd(a^(r/2) - 1, N)

The algorithm's four steps:

  1. Classical preprocessing: Pick a random a < N. Check if gcd(a, N) > 1 — if so, you're done (this is rare but possible).
  2. Quantum period finding: Create a superposition of all x values, compute aˣ mod N in superposition, then apply the QFT to extract the period r from the resulting interference pattern.
  3. Classical postprocessing: Use continued fractions to extract r from the QFT measurement, then compute gcd(a^(r/2) ± 1, N).
  4. Repeat if needed: Some choices of a or measurement outcomes don't yield factors. Repeat with a new random a (~2-3 attempts typically suffice).

Real-world implications:

  • RSA encryption: Based on the assumption that factoring is hard. A large enough quantum computer would break RSA-2048 in hours.
  • Post-quantum cryptography: NIST has already standardized new encryption algorithms (lattice-based, hash-based) that resist quantum attacks.
  • Timeline: Current quantum computers can factor small numbers (15, 21). Factoring RSA-2048 requires ~4,000+ logical qubits with error correction — likely 10-20+ years away, but active research is accelerating.
  • "Harvest now, decrypt later": Adversaries may already be storing encrypted data today to decrypt when quantum computers arrive. This is why the migration to post-quantum cryptography is urgent.
Key Insight: Shor's algorithm is the reason quantum computing matters to global security. It transforms an intractable problem (factoring) into a feasible one, using the QFT to extract hidden periodicity. This single algorithm has reshaped cryptography, national security strategy, and technology investment worldwide.

Noise, Errors & Hardware

Real quantum computers are noisy — understanding errors and hardware is essential for any quantum engineer.

Lesson 6.1

Quantum Noise & Decoherence

Real qubits are extremely fragile. They interact with their environment — stray electromagnetic fields, thermal vibrations, cosmic rays — and lose their quantum properties. This process is called decoherence.

Two types of noise:

  • T₁ (relaxation): The qubit decays from |1⟩ to |0⟩ over time, like a ball rolling downhill. Typical T₁ ≈ 50–300 μs for superconducting qubits.
  • T₂ (dephasing): The qubit loses its phase information (the φ angle on the Bloch sphere randomizes). Usually T₂ ≤ T₁. This destroys interference — the engine of quantum computing.

Common noise models:

  • Bit flip (X error): |0⟩ ↔ |1⟩ with probability p — analogous to classical bit errors
  • Phase flip (Z error): |+⟩ ↔ |−⟩ with probability p — no classical analog
  • Depolarizing: Replaces the qubit with a maximally random state with probability p — the "worst case" noise
  • Amplitude damping: Models energy loss (T₁ decay) — |1⟩ → |0⟩ probabilistically

Gate fidelity: Each gate operation has an error rate. Current hardware achieves ~99.5% for single-qubit gates and ~99% for two-qubit gates. Sounds good, but after 1,000 gates, the accumulated error makes results meaningless.

The coherence budget: You have limited time (T₂) to run your circuit. Each gate takes 20–50 ns. A circuit with 1,000 gates takes ~50 μs — barely within the coherence window. This constrains circuit depth and algorithm design.

Key Insight: Decoherence is the #1 enemy of quantum computing. Every quantum algorithm must finish before the qubits "forget" their quantum state. This time constraint fundamentally shapes what's possible on near-term hardware.
Lesson 6.2

Quantum Error Correction

Classical error correction is simple: copy the bit 3 times (000 or 111) and take a majority vote. But quantum mechanics forbids copying (no-cloning theorem) and measurement destroys the state. So how do we fix errors?

The breakthrough insight: Encode 1 logical qubit into many physical qubits using entanglement, then detect errors by measuring correlations (syndromes) without measuring the data itself.

Key quantum error correction codes:

  • 3-Qubit Bit Flip Code: Encodes |ψ⟩ into 3 qubits. Detects and corrects single X errors. Like classical repetition code, but quantum.
  • Shor's 9-Qubit Code: First code to correct any single-qubit error (X, Y, or Z). Uses 9 physical qubits per logical qubit.
  • Steane Code [[7,1,3]]: 7 physical qubits per logical qubit. More efficient than Shor's code.
  • Surface Code: The leading candidate for real hardware. Qubits arranged on a 2D grid. High error threshold (~1%) — if physical error rates are below 1%, adding more qubits improves reliability. Google, IBM, and others are pursuing this.

The overhead problem:

  • Surface code: ~1,000 physical qubits per logical qubit for useful error correction
  • Running Shor's algorithm on RSA-2048: ~4,000 logical qubits × 1,000 physical = ~4 million physical qubits
  • Current hardware: ~1,000 physical qubits (IBM, Google). The gap is enormous.

Syndrome measurement: Special "ancilla" qubits measure the error syndrome (which errors occurred) without disturbing the logical data. This is done through carefully designed CNOT circuits that extract parity information.

Key Insight: Quantum error correction is what separates toy demos from useful quantum computers. The surface code is the leading approach, but the ~1000x overhead means fault-tolerant quantum computing requires millions of physical qubits.
Lesson 6.3

Quantum Hardware Platforms

There are several competing technologies for building physical qubits. Each has tradeoffs between coherence time, gate fidelity, connectivity, and scalability.

Superconducting Qubits (Google, IBM, Rigetti)

  • Tiny circuits cooled to 15 millikelvin (colder than outer space)
  • Fast gates (~20–50 ns), but short coherence (~100 μs)
  • Limited connectivity (nearest-neighbor on a chip)
  • Currently leading in qubit count (~1,000+)
  • Google's Sycamore achieved "quantum supremacy" (2019)

Trapped Ions (IonQ, Quantinuum, AQT)

  • Individual atoms trapped by electromagnetic fields and manipulated with lasers
  • Excellent coherence (seconds to minutes!)
  • Highest gate fidelities (>99.9% single-qubit, >99.5% two-qubit)
  • All-to-all connectivity (any qubit can interact with any other)
  • Slower gates (~1–100 μs) and harder to scale beyond ~50 qubits currently

Photonic Qubits (Xanadu, PsiQuantum)

  • Qubits are photons (particles of light)
  • Room temperature operation — no cryogenics needed
  • Naturally suited for quantum networking and communication
  • Hard to make photons interact (needed for two-qubit gates)
  • Measurement-based quantum computing model

Other platforms: Neutral atoms (QuEra), topological qubits (Microsoft), silicon spin qubits (Intel), NV centers in diamond. Each is at a different maturity level.

Platform selection: Superconducting for fastest iteration. Trapped ions for highest fidelity. Photonic for quantum networking. Most cloud providers (IBM, Amazon Braket, Azure Quantum) let you try multiple platforms without owning hardware.

Key Insight: There is no single "best" qubit technology yet. The race is still wide open. A quantum engineer should understand the tradeoffs and be platform-aware, not platform-locked.
07

NISQ Era & Applications

What works today on noisy hardware — variational algorithms, quantum ML, and real-world use cases.

Lesson 7.1

Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE)

NISQ (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum) computers can't run Shor's algorithm — they have too few qubits and too much noise. But variational algorithms are designed to work within these constraints.

The VQE idea: Use a quantum computer for what it's good at (exploring quantum state space) and a classical computer for what it's good at (optimization).

  1. Prepare: Build a parameterized quantum circuit (ansatz) with tunable rotation angles θ₁, θ₂, ...
  2. Measure: Run the circuit on quantum hardware, measure the energy ⟨ψ(θ)|H|ψ(θ)⟩
  3. Optimize: Feed the energy to a classical optimizer (gradient descent, COBYLA, etc.) which adjusts the angles
  4. Repeat: Iterate until the energy converges to the ground state

Why it matters: Finding the ground state energy of a molecule is essential for drug discovery, materials science, and chemistry. Classical computers struggle with this for molecules with more than ~50 electrons. VQE could solve this for larger molecules using relatively few qubits.

Current status: VQE has been demonstrated on small molecules (H₂, LiH, BeH₂) on real hardware. Scaling to commercially relevant molecules remains a challenge due to noise and the "barren plateau" problem (gradients vanish in large circuits).

Key Insight: VQE is the hybrid quantum-classical approach that dominates NISQ research. It's "quantum-assisted optimization" — the quantum computer proposes states, the classical computer steers the search.
Lesson 7.2

QAOA — Quantum Approximate Optimization

QAOA (Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm) applies the same hybrid quantum-classical idea to combinatorial optimization problems.

Problems it targets:

  • Max-Cut (graph partitioning)
  • Traveling salesman
  • Portfolio optimization
  • Vehicle routing
  • Scheduling and resource allocation

How QAOA works:

  1. Encode the optimization problem as a Hamiltonian (cost function → quantum operator)
  2. Start all qubits in |+⟩ (equal superposition)
  3. Alternate between "cost" layers (encode the problem) and "mixer" layers (explore solutions), each with tunable angles
  4. Measure → get a candidate solution. Classically optimize the angles. Repeat.

Depth parameter p: More layers (higher p) = better approximation but longer circuits = more noise. Finding the sweet spot is an active research area.

QAOA is the most direct path from quantum hardware to practical value. If quantum advantage is achievable for optimization, QAOA or its variants will likely be the first commercially useful quantum algorithms.

Key Insight: QAOA maps real-world optimization problems onto quantum circuits. It's the leading candidate for near-term quantum advantage in industry — logistics, finance, and supply chain optimization.
Lesson 7.3

Quantum Cryptography (BB84 & QKD)

While Shor's algorithm breaks classical encryption, quantum mechanics also provides unbreakable encryption through Quantum Key Distribution (QKD).

BB84 Protocol (Bennett & Brassard, 1984):

  1. Alice prepares: For each bit, she randomly chooses one of two bases (rectilinear {|0⟩, |1⟩} or diagonal {|+⟩, |−⟩}) and sends a qubit encoding her bit in that basis.
  2. Bob measures: He randomly chooses a basis for each qubit. If he picks the same basis as Alice, he gets the correct bit. If not, he gets a random result.
  3. Sifting: Alice and Bob publicly compare which bases they used (but NOT the bit values). They keep only bits where they chose the same basis — this is the sifted key.
  4. Eavesdropper detection: They sacrifice a random subset of the sifted key and compare values. If an eavesdropper (Eve) intercepted and re-sent qubits, she'd introduce ~25% errors. If error rate is low, the key is secure.

Why it's provably secure: Any measurement by Eve unavoidably disturbs the quantum states (no-cloning + measurement disturbance). Alice and Bob can detect eavesdropping with arbitrary confidence.

Current deployment: QKD is commercially available (ID Quantique, Toshiba). China's Micius satellite demonstrated QKD over 1,200 km. Banks and governments are early adopters. Limited by distance (fiber: ~100 km, satellite: global) and key rates.

Key Insight: QKD is the only encryption method with security guaranteed by the laws of physics, not computational assumptions. It's already deployed commercially — quantum cryptography is not future tech, it's present tech.
Lesson 7.4

Quantum Machine Learning

Quantum Machine Learning (QML) sits at the intersection of two revolutions. The core question: can quantum computers learn patterns faster or find better models than classical ML?

Key QML approaches:

  • Quantum Kernel Methods: Encode classical data into quantum states, then compute inner products (kernels) in the exponentially large Hilbert space. Some datasets may benefit from kernels that are hard to compute classically.
  • Parameterized Quantum Circuits (PQC): The quantum analog of neural networks. Tunable quantum circuits trained via gradient descent. Also called "quantum neural networks."
  • Quantum Boltzmann Machines: Quantum versions of generative models, potentially sampling from distributions that are hard for classical machines.
  • HHL Algorithm: Solves systems of linear equations exponentially faster (with caveats). Linear algebra underlies most of ML — if we could speed it up, the implications would be enormous.

Honest assessment of the current state:

  • No quantum ML algorithm has shown clear practical advantage on real-world data yet
  • The "barren plateau" problem makes training large quantum circuits very difficult
  • Data loading is a bottleneck — encoding classical data into quantum states can negate any speedup
  • Most promising near-term: quantum kernels on specific structured datasets, and quantum-enhanced reinforcement learning

Try PennyLane (Xanadu) or TensorFlow Quantum (Google) to experiment. Understand the theory before expecting practical results. The field is rapidly evolving but still in early research stage.

Key Insight: QML is the most hyped and least proven area of quantum computing. The potential is enormous (exponential feature spaces!), but proven advantages remain elusive. A quantum engineer should understand the theoretical foundations without expecting near-term miracles.
